# Analysis Workflow: Change Point Analysis of Brent Oil Prices
**Birhan Energies — Task 1 Deliverable**

## 1. Objective
Quantify how major geopolitical and economic events have shifted the behavior of
Brent crude oil prices (1987–2022), using Bayesian change point detection, and
translate the results into insights for investors, policymakers, and energy companies.

## 2. Workflow Steps

**Step 1 — Data Ingestion**
Load `BrentOilPrices.csv`, parse `Date` (format `day-month-year`, e.g. `20-May-87`)
into datetime, sort chronologically, and check for gaps, duplicates, and missing values.

**Step 2 — Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)**
- Plot the raw price series to visually locate major regime shifts.
- Compute and plot log returns: `r_t = log(P_t) - log(P_{t-1})` to stabilize variance
  and approximate stationarity.
- Test stationarity on both the raw price and the log-return series using the
  Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test.
- Examine volatility clustering (periods of large swings followed by large swings)
  in the log-return series — this motivates why a single global mean/variance
  model is inadequate and change point / regime-switching models are needed.

**Step 3 — Event Compilation**
Research and compile a structured dataset of 10–15+ major events (wars, sanctions,
OPEC decisions, financial crises) with approximate dates — see
`brent_oil_events.csv`. This will later be cross-referenced against detected
change points.

**Step 4 — Bayesian Change Point Modeling (Task 2)**
- Define a discrete uniform prior over `tau` (the switch point index).
- Define pre-/post-change parameters (e.g., `mu_1`, `mu_2`, and optionally `sigma_1`,
  `sigma_2`).
- Use `pm.math.switch` to select parameters based on whether each time index is
  before or after `tau`.
- Fit with `pm.Normal` likelihood on log returns (or price, depending on the
  formulation) and sample via MCMC (`pm.sample`).

**Step 5 — Model Diagnostics**
Check `r_hat` (~1.0), effective sample size, and trace plots for convergence.
Examine the posterior of `tau` — a sharp peak indicates high confidence in the
detected change point's location.

**Step 6 — Insight Generation**
Match detected change point dates to the closest events in the compiled event
list (within a reasonable window, e.g., ±30–90 days). Quantify the shift
(e.g., "mean daily return moved from X% to Y%," or "average price moved from
$A to $B, a Z% change") and state findings probabilistically, not causally.

**Step 7 — Reporting & Dashboard**
Summarize findings in a report and expose them through the Flask/React
dashboard (Task 3) for interactive exploration by stakeholders.

## 2b. What Are Change Point Models, and Why Use Them Here?

A **change point model** identifies specific points in time where the
statistical properties of a time series shift — for example, the mean, the
variance, or both change abruptly from one regime to another. Instead of
assuming a single distribution generates the entire series, the model
assumes the data is generated by *different* distributions before and after
one or more unknown "switch points."

This matters for Brent oil prices because, as the EDA shows, the series is
clearly **not** a single stable process across 35 years — it moves through
distinct regimes (the $15-20 calm of the 1990s, the 2000s bull run, the 2008
spike/crash, the 2014-16 collapse, the 2020 crash, the 2022 surge). A model
that assumes one constant mean and variance for the whole series would badly
misrepresent all of these periods. A change point model instead lets the
data itself tell us **where** the regime boundaries are, rather than us
having to specify them in advance.

In the Bayesian formulation used here (Task 2), the location of the switch
point `tau` is itself a random variable with a prior distribution (a
discrete uniform over all time indices). Running MCMC sampling produces a
full **posterior distribution** over `tau`, rather than a single point
estimate — this is the key advantage over simpler rule-based change
detection: it gives a principled measure of *how confident* the model is
about *when* the change occurred, and analogously how confident it is about
the size of the shift in the pre/post parameters.

## 2c. Expected Outputs and Their Limitations

A completed Bayesian change point analysis on the Brent oil series is
expected to produce:

- **A posterior distribution over the change point date(s) (`tau`)** — summarized
  as a most-likely date plus a credible interval (e.g., "95% credible interval:
  [date A, date B]"). A narrow, sharply-peaked posterior indicates the model is
  confident about *when* the shift happened; a wide, flat posterior indicates
  the data doesn't clearly support one particular switch date over nearby
  alternatives.
- **Posterior distributions for the "before" and "after" parameters** (e.g., `mu_1`
  vs `mu_2`, and optionally `sigma_1` vs `sigma_2`) — allowing probabilistic
  statements like "there is a 97% probability the mean daily return after the
  change point is higher than before it."
- **A quantified magnitude of change** — e.g., converting the shift in mean
  log-return into an equivalent percentage or dollar move in price level,
  which is the number that gets reported to stakeholders (see Task 2, step 6).

**Limitations of these outputs:**

- A single-`tau` model can only find **one** structural break per run. Since the
  full series clearly has many regime changes, this model needs to be re-run on
  sub-windows, or extended to a multiple-change-point / regime-switching
  formulation, to capture the whole history.
- The output tells us **where and how large** a shift was — it does not, by
  itself, tell us **why** it happened (see the correlation-vs-causation
  discussion in `assumptions_and_limitations.md`). Any event we associate with
  a detected `tau` is a hypothesis based on temporal proximity, not a proven
  cause.
- Posterior estimates are only as good as the model's assumptions (e.g., normal
  likelihood); if the true data-generating process has fatter tails or
  autocorrelated volatility (which the EDA suggests it does), the credible
  intervals may be overconfident.

## 3. Why This Matters
- **Investors** get quantified regime shifts to inform risk models and timing.
- **Policymakers** get evidence linking policy/sanction actions to market response.
- **Energy companies** get a defensible basis for planning and hedging decisions.
